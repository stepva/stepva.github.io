When my best friend and her boyfriend visited me in Vienna after I returned there for my last uni year, they were just coming back from a Baltic vacation and brought me this **Uganda Masira** from Taste Map, a multi-award winning roastery in Vilnius. Not having visited Lithuania yet, it was exciting to at least get a taste of their specialty coffee culture.

From **Mbale**, a city at the bottom of the slopes of **Mount Elgon**, an extinct shield volcano in Uganda, the coffee was grown by an award-winning farmer Dison Kareng and fermented naturally and anaerobically without oxygen in air-tight containers. It is a 100% Arabica, a mixture of three varieties - SL28, one of the most well-known African varieties, originated in Kenya but increasingly important in Uganda; SL14; and SL34, all three coming from the Scott Agricultural Laboratories from the 1930s, one of the main centers for coffee breeding in East Africa, established by the colonial British government in Kenya in 1922.

![Mbale, Uganda]({{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/mbale_uganda.png "Mbale, Uganda")

This was a great coffee, I really, really enjoyed it. Already in the beans I could smell dried fruits, particularly **raisins**, which were also the main taste for me in most of the brews. The official taste profile includes them, alongside **plums, dried fruits and berries**, finishing with "complex, juicy, aromatic, vinous", which goes hand in hand with the raisins.

It tasted the best during the first month since I got it, when I mostly used a basic french press method. Later on I think it got a bit more bitter and the dried fruit taste was not that strong, I then experiment a bit with some Hario V60 methods, variants of the basic 4:6 method, and I managed to get a bunch of last good brews with shorter pouring intervals (around 30 seconds). Nevertheless, I will always remember the first few cups, as I love raisins.
