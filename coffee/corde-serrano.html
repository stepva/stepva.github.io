At the beginning of February, I visited my friend who's working at CERN, in Geneva, Switzerland. It was a great vacation, I got some absolutely crazy and interesting tours of CERN (I finally properly understand what they do there!) and we also did some skiing in France. On the days when he had to work, I wandered through Geneva - since it was not my first time there, I didn't have to tick off all the touristy stuff and could instead for example just chill in cafÃ©s. One of them was Corde Coffee, a very cute cafÃ© (with delicious pastry as well!), where I had a cup of Cuba Serrano, which I then also bought after a nice chat with the barista and the owner.

Unfortunately, I'm not able to trace the coffee that much, but I did discuss it with the owner who is very passionate about bringing more Cuban coffee to Geneva and is talking directly with the producer. What I figured is that the Serrano is one of the main coffee "types" in Cuba, this one was grown on the slopes of Sierra Maestra, a mountain range in southeast Cuba and one of the main coffee-growing locations on the island. It's a mix of two classic Arabica varieties, Typica and Bourbon, and was processed with the washed method. According to Corde Coffee, it achieved a specialty cupping score of 84.

The taste profile already suggested more of a darker roast and a "classical" coffee taste - dark chocolate, roasted hazelnut, and cardamom, with little acidity. I brewed it mostly with the V60 at home in Prague during my February holidays, and my mom really enjoyed this one. However, the preparation was really interesting. First of all, A LOT of ground coffee was always left in the insides of the grinder, like a really large amount. Again, I sort of expected it from the washed coffees, but this one was extreme. Also during the bloom phase, when the coffee first comes into contact with water, it really puffed up, like a dough in the oven, something I have never seen before. It was happening with every brew and it was very strange, I wasn't even able to swirl it.

I experimented a bit with the recipes later on, but this "issue" persisted. Taste-wise, however, it didn't seem to matter. It was good, the taste profile matched exactly and we would really taste the dark coffee and nuts. As I already said, my mom was a huge fan. I also managed to experiment a bit with the AeroPress "espresso-like" brews, and it was successful, with a full and aromatic taste. But now that I'm writing this, I've decided that I will really try to investigate the weird "puffing up" more.
