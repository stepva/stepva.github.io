Before our small Malta getaway, I had done some research about the specialty cafÃ©s and roasters on the island, naturally planning to buy some beans. My idea was to buy them in the capital, Valletta, where the most cafÃ©s are located, but to my surprise, already on the second day I randomly stumbled upon a great place in Victoria, Gozo, called Victoria Central, home of Vicky Coffee, a local roastery. Thanks to the amazing staff and great tiramisu, it didn't take long to change my plans and buy coffee there. Running short on time and with some help from the baristas, I decided to go for this Microlot #3.

Farmed in Nyarabuye (or Nyarubuye), Rwanda, the infamous location of the 1994 massacre between Tutsis and Hutu, it luckily tastes better than this history. However, the Nyarubuye Washing station, where this coffee was probably processed (although it isn't washed, but processed naturally), is closely linked to the genocide, with the founder and owner of the Mahembe company deciding to invest in the community by building a wet mill after the unfortunate event.

This coffee is produced by Coffee Villages, a group connecting over 1,200 small local farmers, focusing on providing a clean water infrastructure to the farmers as well as educational programs for women and children. Run by Tom Bagaza, it is a fascinating and inspiring project which I recommend checking out. Like most of the local coffees, this micro-lot is a Red Bourbon, grown between 1700 and 2000 MASL.

Once again, this was a coffee with a very nice and strong sweet, fruity smell. The official notes say blackberry, pineapple, passion fruit, grapes, and orange blossoms, suggesting a more acidic and complex flavour profile. I bought the coffee already pretty late after its roasting on the 18th of January, and continued to drink it well into 2 months after roasting, but luckily it did not seem to matter that much. I used almost only Hario V60 and I enjoyed the results.

It was also this coffee with which I started experimenting with ChatGPT - trying to find the right prompt combination to get an "ideal" recipe from ChatGPT-4, hopefully focusing on the full complexity of the process. It was (and still is) an interesting idea and I enjoy it. Trying it with different coffees, it does seem to correctly pick up some aspects of the rost, such as the darkness or the taste profile, suggesting some specific points like higher water temperature or faster pouring to highlight the fruity notes more. This is something I want to keep exploring and I'll be trying it with all my coffee from now.
